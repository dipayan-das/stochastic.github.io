The University of Tulsa Hand

• Version 1

This is a framework for underactuated robotic hands whose fingers are coupled to the actuators by a compliant mechanism. The framework is based on modeling the compliant mechanism as a multiport network and analyzing the eigenvectors of the network's stiffness matrix to describe the way changes in actuator effort affect the contact force on a grasped object. An example of such a multiport compliant mechanism based on a coil spring and finger return springs is presented and evaluated analytically and in experiment. This mechanism forms the core of the TU Hand, an anthropomorphic, two-actuator robotic hand that is capable of coordinating the movements of its four fingers through superposition of two synergies which may be activated individually or in concert.

The compliance and synergistic actuation of the TU hand allow it to conform to the grasped object and deliberately vary the contact forces once grasped. If a different set of synergies is desired, one can simply change the pattern with which the finger tendons are tied to the coils of the spring to produce them

It is able to do this through the use of its two synergies, which may be activated independently or jointly. Despite its adaptability, the hand has limitations. Its compliant mechanism enables it to perform the selected synergies by distributing the actuator forces to each of the fingers.

• Iros Grasping Challenge 2017

Guided by the notion that the five-fingered anthropomorphic hand is a good general purpose manipulator, Team Tulsa approached the hand-in-hand portion of the grasping and manipulation competition using a simplified anthropomorphic hand. The hand had a simplified thumb, fixed in the opposed position, and only two actuators. Motions of the fingers and thumb were coupled together using a ``ties and skips'' architecture where thumb and finger tendons were tied to specific coils of a ``mainspring'' in a manner that produced the best behavior across the wide range of challenges.

The actuators could move or deform the spring in common mode, which resulted in an enveloping grasp) or differential mode (which resulted in a pinch grasp) and superimpose the two modes. The compliant nature of the hand allowed the fingers to conform to the the object as the grasp was acquired. This strategy allowed the retrieval of all objects from the basket (all on the first or second attempt by the volunteer), and scooping peas from the dish, but could not operate the hammer (due to its weight) the syringe, or the scissors (as they required increased dexterity)

• Version 3

Underactuation is a vital strategy to keep robotic hands lightweight, less complicated to operate and more user-friendly. We tried to make a robot hand with a novel compliant transmission mechanism with only two motors that can control up to 16 tendons. We have enhanced the grasping capability of a dual motor under actuated hand which was previously capable of only power grasp to capable of achieving both power and parallel grasp by underactuating the Interossi tendon as well as the flexor tendon, thus making the hand capable of Parallel Grasp.

Modified ties and skip network with a spring conneting 2 non adjacent nodes

We will discuss the theoretical details and main motivation behind creating a network of compliant elements that transmit the actuator efforts to the finger tendons. We will also review the design of the actuator prototype which is an implementation of a ``spider-web'' compliant network and how it has been implemented on the TU Hand V2. The control method used to actuate the hand is also described. The hand using this compliant transmission is capable of grasping objects of different sizes and shapes with a variety of grasp poses.
